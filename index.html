Did You Ever Notice?

Unimportant questions about why Seinfeld wasn’t funny the first few seasons.

By Anthony D'Arrigo

July 10, 2022

Jerry Seinfeld No GIFfrom Jerry Seinfeld GIFs

Jerry in season 5. Speechelss, yet funny



Seinfeld, objectively the funniest show ever made, wasn’t popular the first few seasons because, well, it wasn’t very funny. As Jerry Seinfeld himself has observed, the show probably wouldn’t have survived today because it wasn’t an immediate hit. The show’s quirky timing and distinctive voice didn’t develop until the third or fourth season.

Shallow, fairly obvious questions

Analyzing dialogue from each episode’s script, by character and by season, what can we learn about how the show evolved, from no buzz (circa seasons 1-3) to TV darling (seasons 5-9)?

1. How did each character’s share of dialogue change as the show progressed from season to season? Did Jerry initially have an outsized share of the dialogue?

2. Did the writers reach some magic equilibrium/balance in terms of how they shared dialogue and/or distributed storylines between the four main characters?

Shallow, less obvious observations

Seinfeld was funnier the less Jerry talked.

Now, in Jerry's defense, the first two seasons were not the traditional 22 episodes. Not even close. Season one was limited to just four episodes and season two only 12. Nonetheless, the shows popularity increased as Jerry’s share of the show’s dialogue declined, from 54% the first season, to 22% the final season. Sorry Jerry.



...so if Jerry’s share of dialogue declined so sharply, who talked more? Hmm, well it wasn’t Georgie boy; his share steadily declined after season 2, especially after season 5…



...Elaine, of course Elaine! Wait. No, it’s not Elaine; her share in season 2 is nearly identical to season 9…



...Kramer! I knew it. More Kramer. Less Jerry. From 5% season 1, to about 17% season 9; passing Elaine and nearly matching George. Giddy up!



But Kramer's rise isn't the whole story...

The sweet spot is not a myth

Seinfeld’s writers/producers (accidentally?) discovered the sweet spot in season 5.

The consistency in how they distributed lines among the four main characters over seasons 5-9, when Seinfeld was the top-rated prime time show is remarkable: Jerry, George, Elaine, and Kramer shared, in aggregate, nearly 70% of the dialogue every season (70.8%, 71.8%, 67.6%, 70.7%, 71.5%); in contrast to the first three seasons, when it was 83%. So if the share of the main players declined the final five seasons, what happened? Who emerged?

Well, no one. Outside the four main players, no other character consumed more than 3% of the dialogue. Even Susan (before her untimely death) maxed out at 2.5% in season 7. Even when we add up the remaining cast of recurring characters—Newman, Jerry’s parents, George’s parents, Putty, Susan, yada yada yada—it peaks at 10%. The beauty of the show was how non-recurring characters, who only appeared in one or two episodes, were, well, so funny!
